Astronomers

A one-page roleplaying game about discovering planets

You sit at a comptuer terminal, recieving feedback from your telescope and deepspace satellite in a remote location. You crack your knuckles, poised over the keyboard. There are entire galaxies out there, you just know it.

What you'll need to play

  • A standard 52-card deck
  • A six-sided die
  • Post-its
  • Pencil
  • Workspace

How you play

Goal

Astronomers is a cooperative roleplaying game. The goal is to discover all the nearby planets and stars, and discover the stories of those out of your world.

At the end of the game, you'll have discovered stories about planets no one's ever discovered. This works as a great world-building exercise for any role-playing game.

Getting Ready

Turn order is determined with whoever looked up at the stars most recently going first. If there's a tie, then it's whoever can point closest to North. Then play continues clockwise.

Players start by shuffling the deck. Each card will represent a celestial body, so the deck itself represents all the observable celestial objects in this corner of the universe from your vantage point with your telescope. Similarly, the workspace represents the scope of your telescope since the workspace is the only place you can put cards during the game.

After the deck is shuffled, hand it to the player going first.

On your turn

  1. Deal one card from the deck face down. The card is normally from the top of the deck but it can come from anywhere in the deck. This represents the randomness and chaos of the universe.
  2. Place the card anywhere on the workspace and roll the dice.
  3. Flip over the card and, using the dice and card value tables listed below, determine what type of celestial object you've just discovered.
  4. Give a quick overview of your object, simply outlining what kind of object it is, how big it is, and what its environment is like.
  5. Then it's time for the Press Room. All other players can ask the current player up to 2 questions to learn more about the object they just discovered. Whatever that player says is the final word on the celestial object. There are some sample questions below.
  6. Make sure you take notes about the celestial object on the post-it note so all players remember what the object is.
  7. Your turn is over, play continues clockwise.

Revisiting previous discoveries

If a player believes there's more to be discovered about a celestial object than initially thought, they can revisit a celestial object.

Now that player draws a card, but must place it on top of the current card on the workspace that they want to revisit. Now that celestial object has both attributes, and the Press Room reopens for questions from other players.

If the card drawn is the same value as the old card, that means the attribute is doubled. For example, if a player drew the Two of Hearts then revisited it and drew the Two of Spades, and "two" represents "fire", then that object could potentially explode, go supernova, etc; basically, the fire is much stronger than initially anticipated.

This is a very loose mechanic, and players are welcome to adapt it to how they see fit. Maybe if a distant spaceship was discovered, perhaps it's moved to somewhere else in the workspace, etc.

Ending the game

Gameplay ends once all the cards are drawn.

In the end, the workspace could be covered in cards and post-it notes as well as stories that span solar systems.

Players are encouraged to recount the tales they told. The map can then even work for a table-top RPG in space!

Appendix

Tables

Table of card values: Use the table below to determine the attribute of the celestial object you drew for.

Value Attribtue
ACE Icy, cold
2 (Two) Literally on fire
3 (Three) Made of diamond or other rare material
4 (Four) In the Goldilocks zone
5 (Five) Rotating quickly
6 (Six) Completely covered in water
7 (Seven) Hollow
8 (Eight) Desert, arid
9 (Nine) Split in half
10 (Ten) Going supernova
Jack Very flat
Queen Oddly shaped
King Player's choice / make one up

Table of dice values: Use the table below to determine the size of the celestial object from a d6.

Dice roll Size
1 (One) Tiny meteoroid
2 (Two) Space-ship sized
3 (Three) Large meteoroid
4 (Four) Small-ish planet
5 (Five) Giant planet
6 (Six) Star

Press Room Questions (samples / examples)

  • I see that this planet is in the Goldilocks Zone. Are there any civilizations on it, or is it all plantlife?
  • These two celestial objects are really close to each other. How do they impact each other's gravities? Do they have cultural impacts on each other? Can you see any spaceships flying from one to another?
  • Can you determine the trajectory of the huge spaceship you just discovered?
  • Where does it look like that meteorite is headed?
